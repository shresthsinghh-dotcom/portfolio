Adaptive Ball Launcher â€“ Human-Centered Mechanical Design Project

I designed and prototyped an electronically operated, adaptive ball launcher intended to enable independent play for children with motor impairments. The goal of this project was to create a solution that was not only functional, but accessible, affordable, durable, and easily replicable using off-the-shelf components. I began by applying the engineering design process, defining the problem through user and client analysis with input from caregivers and pediatric occupational therapists. From this, I translated qualitative user needs into quantitative design criteria, including activation force, launch distance, adaptability to different projectiles, durability, modularity, and cost constraints. I used pairwise comparison methods to prioritize these criteria and guide design trade-offs. I then designed the mechanical system in Onshape, developing a modular, spring-actuated mechanism that converts stored potential energy into a consistent launch motion. I integrated electronic control using Arduino, allowing for low-force activation suitable for users with limited motor control. Throughout development, I applied tolerancing principles, documented decisions through technical memos, and iteratively refined the design based on testing data. I conducted quantitative and qualitative testing to validate performance, achieving consistent launch accuracy and stable operation across multiple conditions. This project strengthened my skills in mechanical system design, prototyping, human-centered engineering, technical documentation, and iterative testing, while reinforcing the importance of designing technology that is both effective and inclusive.
